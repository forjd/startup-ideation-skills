# Startup Problem Capture

## When to use

Use when the user mentions a startup idea, product idea, workflow pain, market frustration, repeated annoyance, manual process, expensive workaround, or asks whether something could be a business.

Do not start by designing the product. First capture the problem in a way that can be challenged.

## Core rule

Keep the user anchored on the pain, the person feeling it, the moment it happens, and the cost. Avoid turning every frustration into a SaaS idea by default.

## Workflow

1. Ask for the raw problem in the user's words if it is not already clear.
2. Fill the problem canvas below using known information.
3. Mark unknowns as `Unknown`, not guessed facts.
4. Ask only the highest-value missing questions first. Prefer 3–5 questions, not a giant form.
5. Reflect back a sharper problem statement.
6. If the problem is vague, split it into candidate problem statements instead of forcing one.

## Problem canvas

Use this format:

```markdown
Problem:
Who has it:
When it happens:
Current workaround:
Cost of the problem:
How often it happens:
Who pays:
Existing alternatives:
Why now:
Could I reach 10 users:
Could I build v1 in 2–4 weeks:
```

## Question prompts

Use plain, direct questions such as:

- Who exactly has this problem? Be narrower than an industry if possible.
- What are they trying to do when the problem appears?
- What do they do today instead?
- What does the workaround cost in time, money, risk, lost revenue, stress, or compliance exposure?
- How often does it happen per person, team, or business?
- Who has budget or authority to pay for a fix?
- What tools, agencies, spreadsheets, scripts, or internal processes already compete with this?
- Why is this more urgent now than two years ago?
- Could you realistically speak to 10 people with this problem in the next fortnight?
- Could a v1 prove value in 2–4 weeks without building a platform?


## Quality bar

A captured problem is good enough to grill when:

- The user segment is specific enough to find real people.
- The triggering situation is concrete.
- The workaround is known or explicitly unknown.
- The cost is plausible and testable.
- There is a credible buyer or a clear reason the user is not yet sure who pays.

## Pitfalls

- Do not invent market size, pricing, or customer behaviour.
- Do not praise the idea as good before the problem is clear.
- Do not optimise for a polished pitch. Optimise for something falsifiable.
- Do not ask all questions every time. Ask the questions that change the next decision.
